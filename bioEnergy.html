The transformation from biomass to biofuel is an extensive and encouraging step towards the development of future sustainable energy solutions. This renewable and alternative energy provides a solution to several environmental, energy, and waste management issues. Created from organic materials such as crops, agricultural residues, forestry byproducts, and organic waste, biofuel provides a diverse number of options for harvesting renewable energy.

Solid biofuels, such as wood pellets and agricultural residues like straw and corn stover(stalks/stems, leaves, unused parts of plants, find common use in residential, commercial, and industrial heating applications. Liquid biofuels, notably ethanol and biodiesel, are considered a greener substitute for gasoline and diesel in the transportation sector. Ethanol, primarily produced through the fermentations found in crops, is blended with gasoline to reduce emissions and enhance octane ratings. Biodiesel, synthesized and produced from vegetable oils or animal fats, offers a clean-burning alternative to diesel fuel, contributing to air quality improvement and dependence on finite petroleum resources. Biogas, the third major sector of biofuels, is mainly generated through anaerobic digestion of organic matter. Composed of primary methane and carbon dioxide, biogas can be utilized in many applications including heating, electricity generation, and as a clean fuel for vehicles.




These different types of biofuel share a common similarity, being derived from organic resources that provide a better alternative than their limited counterparts. With many benefits involved with biofuels, there also lies an extensive process used to produce these alternative fuels. In addition to the many benefits of biofuel technology, biofuel is a possible sustainable answer to waste management problems; controlling and disposing of organic waste out of the landfill, and reducing greenhouse gas. Other examples will be provided in later information, including the process for solid biofuels, liquid biofuels, and biogasses. With many complex processes, organic resources that are often seen as waste can be renewed and turned into more sustainable energy, that provides efficient and cost-effective energy.[1]



Benefits & Limitations of Biofuel

The production of biofuel can greatly differ, depending on which fuel is desired. For the process of liquid biofuels, such as biodiesel, or bio-ethanol, the process often involves obtaining its original component, oftentimes used as vegetable oils or animal fat. For biogas, the main component mainly includes any biological wet waste that can be decomposed through anaerobic digestion. In this process, bacteria are able to digest the waste in low-oxygen environments, creating gasses such as methane and carbon dioxide, which can both be used and harvested to produce energy and electricity. As for solid biofuels, the main component originally comes from solid organic biomass such as plants, animal waste, and organic municipal waste. From these three methods, they share a similar advantage. These processes often provide effective use of biomass resources, which address waste management issues, whilst promoting a sustainable environment.[2] As compared to common greenhouse gasses, biomethane can reduce up to 30 to 70 percent of average greenhouse gas emissions. [3]



Despite this, the process of converting these fuels to usable energy often requires combustion; there is a significant decrease in emissions of greenhouse gasses compared to its fossil fuel counterparts. Compared to natural methane, the biomethane created from biogas has great potential as a clean energy source for many appliances including heating, power generation, and automobile fuel. Compared to common greenhouse gasses, biomethane can reduce up to 30 to 86 percent of average greenhouse gas emissions in the US. [4] This can help mitigate air pollution and lessen the carbon footprint involved in its production. This energy source, biofuel, is very flexible as it can be used for automobile fuel, heat production, and electricity. Since organic waste is common and can often be found in various regions, biofuel systems provide chances for localized energy production, enabling local communities to be more energy-independent than on centralized power networks. [5]



Yet despite the benefits of a greener environment and flexibility, there are certain limitations on the production of biofuels. The main drawback of the product is the need for a steady supply of organic feedstock to maintain production. Though this may not often be an issue for highly populated areas, localized regions may have issues meeting the quotas required to maintain positive energy production. Similarly, proper planning and management are needed due to the potential impact of feedstock composition and availability. Most importantly, biofuel infrastructure may result in financial difficulties due to the initial creation and outgoing expense, especially on small scale. or resource-constrained organizations. Yet despite these fuels being created from organic waste, there is still the possibility of contaminating the environment. Improper biofuel production may produce odors and require proper, cautious disposal of digestate by-products. [6]



Chemcial Reactions/ Processes

The production of solid biofuels primarily involves the collection and processing of biomass materials. As mentioned previously, these materials may range from organic materials such as wood chips, agricultural residue, and other solid organic waste. Once collected, the materials undergo a drying process such as torrefaction or pyrolysis, which involves heating the biomass to temperatures between 200 and 300 C in the absence of oxygen to reduce its moisture content. This reduction of moisture expels volatile molecules and allows the biomass to be more efficient for combustion. Then, the dried biomass is subjected to processes such as pelletization of briquetting. The dried biomass is then subjected to mechanical processes such as grinding or chopping to create manageable feedstock. Under this process, the dried product is compressed into small dense pellets or briquettes, that are easy to store, transport, and use. Pelletizing involves extruding the biomass through a small hole, and then under high pressure and heat, compact pellets are formed. Briquetting compresses the dried biomass to a larger, and denser block, using a combination of pressure and binding agents, to hold the biomass together. These solid biofuels produce energy under combustion; the solid biomass reacts rapidly with oxygen releasing carbon dioxide, water, and energy. The process of this can be seen with the following equation: [7]




CH1.44O0.66 + 1.03O2 → 0.72H2O + CO2 (+Heat)




The equation above shows the process of combustion of biomass. The first reactant CH1.44O0.66 represents the organic compound undergoing combustion. This reactant is considered to be the approximate combustible portion of biomass. However, this chemical equation would slightly vary depending on the materials used during the process of combustion, resulting in a differing amount of energy. The second reactant Oxygen represents the current oxygen from the surrounding environment. These reactants combined with a high temperature environment of above 600 C, form the following products of water vapor and carbon dioxide. Most importantly the (+Heat) shows that the combustion reaction is exothermic, releasing heat energy also as a product of the reaction. [8] This heat generated can then be used and turned into usable energy through steam turbines, CHP systems, or ORC systems. The energy generated from the heat from the biomass can then be converted into electricity for a variety of uses. Today, solid biofuels are typically used in heating stems, or power plants for the generation of electricity. [9]




The production of ethanol is commonly produced through the fermentation of sugars present in crops; ex: corn, and sugarcane. This process first begins with the extraction of sugar from the biomass; once extracted, the sugar is fermented by microorganisms, generally bacteria and yeast, to produce ethanol and carbon dioxide. For the process of corn ethanol production, the corn is first milled and processed to release the starch, allowing enzymes to convert it to simple sugars. Yeast is then added to the sugar solution, which is then fermented into ethanol and carbon dioxide.




C6H12O6 → 2C2H5OH + 2CO2 + 2 ATP




This is the basic reaction of the fermentation of sugar. [10] The reactant or substrate for fermentation in this equation is glucose. This molecule is further broken down under the anaerobic condition mentioned above. As a result, Ethanol (2C2H5OH) is created as a product of fermentation, with Carbon Dioxide (2CO2) released as a byproduct and waste during the breakdown of glucose. This process typically requires anaerobic conditions for fermentation and often results in a mixture that contains 10-15% ethanol. (Ethanol Production) Another process mentioned previously, transesterification, is often used to produce biodiesel from vegetable oils and animal fats. In this process, the fats or oils react with alcohol (often methanol) in the presence of a catalyst from biodiesel and glycerol. This process can be simplified to triglyceride (fat/oil) + methanol → biodiesel + glycerol.

This reaction leads to the separation of the biodiesel and glycerol, with the biodiesel floating on the top of the solution, with the glycerol settling at the bottom, allowing easy collection of the biodiesel. [11]Under further processes such as purification and cleaning, the biodiesel is purified to remove any residual contaminants. These biofuels, unlike their counterparts, produce lower greenhouse emissions of up to 74%.[12] [13]



Biogasses require certain oxygen conditions in which a biological process occurs in the absence of oxygen, where microorganisms break down organic matter into biogas, primarily consisting of methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2), along with trace amounts of other gasses. [14]. Currently, transportation contributes to roughly 29% of the total greenhouse emissions in the US. With the addition of biofuel sources, the total greenhouse emission from transportation can be drastically decreased by up to 6%[15]. This process of producing biogas, like the previous fuels, requires collecting materials which can include agricultural waste, manure, municipal waste, plant material, sewage, or food waste. These materials are then placed into a digester tank, where conditions including temperature and pH are controlled to optimize the activity of anaerobic bacteria. With a temperature range of around 31 to 34 C and a pH range of between 7.6 and 8.0, the digestion process begins and is separated into four major stages: hydrolysis, acidogenic, acetogenesis, and methanogenesis. [16] Hydrolysis, the initial stage involves complex organic compounds found in the original biomass such as carbohydrates, proteins, and fats are broken down into simpler soluble molecules. Enzymes produced by hydrolytic bacteria catalyze this process. In this stage, these compounds are broken down further, with carbohydrates broken into simple sugars, proteins into amino acids, and fats into fatty acids and glycerol. This process is crucial for the following stages as it breaks down polymers (large molecules) into monomers that are accessible and able to be used by other bacteria in the following stages.[17] [18]



Hydrolysis
C6H10O4 + 2H2O → C6H12O6 + 2H2



The second stage, acidogenesis uses the previously soluble products from hydrolysis, in which acidogenic/ fermentive bacteria further ferment and break down the simpler molecules. These bacteria ferment the molecules into volatile fatty acids (VFA) such as acetic acid, propionic acid, and butyric acid. This fermentation also produces alcohol, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide. As a result, the pH level of the digestion tank decreases due to the accumulation of these acids.


Acidogenesis
C3H7O2N + 2H2O → C2H4O2 + NH3 + CO2 + 2H2 + ATP
C4H9O3N + H2O → C3H6O2 + NH3 + CO2 + H2 + ATP
4CH3COCOO− + 4H2O → 5CH3COO− + 2HCO3− + 3H+



The third stage acetogenesis requires acetogenic bacteria to convert the products of acidogenic (VFA, alcohols, and other products) into into acetic acids, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide. These three products are the main components used in the final stage of methanogenesis. This third stage serves as a link between acidogenesis and methanogenesis, as it ensures that non-methanogenic substrates are transformed into substances that can be utilized by methanogens.[19]



Acetogenesis
CH3CH2COO− + 3H2O ⇌ CH3COO− + H+ + HCO3− + 3H2
C6H12O6 + 2H2O ⇌ 2CH3COOH + 2CO2 + 4H2
CH3CH2OH + 2H2O ⇌ CH3COO− + 2H2 + H−





Once the substrates are transformed, the final stage occurs, methanogenesis. This final stage of anaerobic digestion involves methanogenic archaea converting the previous stage's acetic acid, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide into methane and carbon dioxide, which therefore creates/biogas. At this stage, there are two main pathways for methane production, including acetoclastic methanogenesis where methane is produced by splitting acetic acids into methane and carbon dioxide, and hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis, in which methane can be produced from carbon dioxide and hydrogen. These four stages all are sensitive processes, greatly influenced by the digester's environmental conditions.[20] [21]This entire process may range from a few weeks to a few months depending on the materials digest. The biogas produced from this process can be used as many sources of energy such as fuel for heating, electricity generation, for vehicle fuel. Furthermore, the residual digestate produced by this process can often serve as a nutrient-rich fertilizer, further promoting a better eco-friendly environment.[22] Biofuel is a possible sustainable answer to waste management problems; controlling and disposing of organic waste out of the landfills, and reducing greenhouse gas.

Converting From Biofuel to Electricity

As mentioned above, the conversion of solid biofuels, such as wood pellets/ agricultural residue requires combustion in which they are burned in the presence of oxygen to release heat. Once the solid biofuel becomes ignited, and provided enough oxygen, an oxidation reaction occurs. This breaks down the solid fuel, breaking down complex organic molecules into simpler compounds, releasing heat energy. This heat generated from the combustion can then be used to produce electricity, by using mechanical energy. The heat can be used to produce steam, which can then generate mechanical energy through turbines, or electrical generators. The pressure from the increasing amount of steam in turbines converts the thermal energy of steam to mechanical energy which is then transformed into electricity through electromagnetic induction in generators. [23]





However, the conversion of liquid biofuels such as ethanol or biodiesel follows a different process for the conversion of fuel into electricity. The process by which liquid biofuels can be converted is known as gasification, producing biogas. This biogas can then be converted into electricity. Gasification is a process that requires the partial oxidation of the liquid biofuel in a high-temperature environment, in the presence of steam and oxygen. (Thermochemical) For simplicity, gasification involves the breakdown of liquid biofuel molecules into components through pyrolysis( decomposition of organic material in an oxygen-deficient environment under high temperatures), reforming, and water-gas shift reactions (chemical reaction in which CO reacts with H2O to produce CO2 and H2 gas under high temperature). As a result of gasification, the liquid biofuels are now a gaseous-like mixture known as syngas, which is further processed to remove impurities, while increasing methane content, thus creating biogas.[24] Like solid biofuel conversion, biogas can be converted into electricity by combustion. However, prior to conversion, the biogas requires extensive purification such as scrubbing, adsorption, and membrane filtration to increase the methane content. Once purified, the gas can be used to generate electricity through combustion in gas engines or turbines. Like prior methods, mechanical energy requires pressure, continuous movement, and heat.[25] [26]





The electrical output of a biofuel greatly varies depending on many factors including its type, quality, amount, as well as efficiency of conversion. Assuming that the solid biofuel is being converted to electrical energy by combustion, and mainly consists of plant-based components such as wood; averages around 4.63- 5.1229 kWh per kg. However, this output will vary depending on the materials/composition used as well as the moisture content of the solid biofuel. Similarly, the electrical output will also vary for liquid biofuels such as ethanol and biodiesel, depending on temperature, and quality. From an average value of various sources found online, biodiesel outputs an estimated 7.8944 kWh per kg . Ethanol produces roughly 6.5 MWh - 7.444 MWh per kg. Similarly, biogas output will also vary depending on the quality and materials used before production. Since biogas is a gas, the energy output would be measured in terms of the volume of cubic meters. The output for biogas ranges from 2.14 - 6 kWh per cubic meter of biogas (commonly biomethane).[27] [28] [29] [30] [31]
